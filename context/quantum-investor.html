The Madness of People Is Quantum

Two famous quotes — one from Newton, one from Feynman — sound unrelated. They're actually the same sentence. And resolving them is real, published science you can run on a few qubits.

Isaac Newton · 1720

"I can calculate the motion of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people."

…said after losing a fortune in the South Sea Bubble.
⟷
Richard Feynman

"If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics."

…classical intuition is the wrong tool for some systems.

01The idea in one breath

If you remember nothing else, remember this:

Newton couldn't predict the “madness of people” for the exact same reason Feynman says you can't grasp quantum mechanics classically: human decision-making doesn't obey classical probability — it obeys quantum probability.

Newton tried to apply heavenly (deterministic, classical) mechanics to a system that is fundamentally quantum-like. The “madness” isn't random noise to average away — it's structured non-classical behaviour. Feynman tells us why classical intuition was doomed; the science of quantum cognition tells us what to use instead.

02Why human choices look “quantum”

People break the rules of classical probability in three signature ways — and these are exactly the fingerprints of quantum probability. You don't need any physics to feel why.

Order effects

Order changes the answer

Ask “Is the market safe?” then “Should you invest?” and you get different answers than asking them in the reverse order. In classical probability, order can't matter. In quantum, it's natural — measurements that don't commute.

Interference

Uncertainty isn't an average

People who'd invest after good news and after bad news often won't invest when the news is unknown. Classically impossible. Quantum-ly, the two possibilities interfere and cancel.

Context

The question creates the answer

Preferences aren't fixed values waiting to be read — how you ask constructs the choice. That's exactly what a quantum measurement does: it collapses a superposition.

Visual: why “order matters” = “the axes are tilted”

Each question is a direction. Answering = projecting your mental state onto that direction. Project onto A then B, vs B then A — because the arrows are tilted (non-commuting), you land in different places. That tilt is the whole trick.

Visual: interference of intentions → a bubble

When many investors' “intentions” line up, their quantum waves add constructively — a bubble. When they clash, they cancel — a crash of confidence. Drag the slider: this is herding as interference.

04The papers, in detail (math & data)

Foundation · the shared formalism

Quantum probability as a model of judgement

Busemeyer & Bruza, Quantum Models of Cognition and Decision, Cambridge Univ. Press (2012)

Replace classical (Kolmogorov) probability with quantum probability. A belief is a vector; an answer is a projection; the order of projections matters when they don't commute.

// belief state — a unit vector in a Hilbert space ℋ
|ψ⟩ ∈ ℋ,   ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1

// an answer = a subspace with projector P (P² = P = P†). Born rule:
Pr(answer) = ‖ P|ψ⟩ ‖² = ⟨ψ| P |ψ⟩

// ask A then B (with collapse) — sequential measurement
Pr(A=a, then B=b) = ‖ PbB · PaA |ψ⟩ ‖²

// classical iff the projectors commute; otherwise ORDER MATTERS
[PA, PB] = PAPB − PBPA = 0 ⇒ joint distribution exists (classical)
[PA, PB] ≠ 0 ⇒ no joint, order effects, interference

// interference: two amplitudes for one outcome don't just add as probabilities
Pr = |a₁ + a₂|² = |a₁|² + |a₂|² + 2·Re(a₁* a₂)  ← no classical analogue

// deliberation = unitary evolution under a cognitive Hamiltonian H
|ψ(t)⟩ = e−iHt |ψ(0)⟩

Two questions are compatible (commute → classical) or incompatible (non-commuting → quantum). Human attitude questions behave as incompatible — that's the whole game.

The killer evidence · parameter-free

The QQ-equality for question-order effects

Wang, Solloway, Shiffrin & Busemeyer, PNAS 111(26):9431 (2014)

Derives an identity the quantum model must satisfy regardless of any parameter, then confirms it on real surveys. This is what separates a model from a metaphor.

// four sequential probabilities per question pair (y = yes, n = no)
q = [ Pr(Ay,Bn) + Pr(An,By) ] − [ Pr(By,An) + Pr(Bn,Ay) ] = 0
// i.e. the chance of giving DIFFERENT answers is the same in both orders

// why it must hold: law of reciprocity of transition probabilities
|⟨ai|bj⟩|² = |⟨bj|ai⟩|²  ⇒ q = 0 for ANY state, ANY questions
Domain input
70 national surveys (Gallup / Pew). Each has two yes/no attitude questions asked in counterbalanced order (half see A first, half B first); large n per survey → the eight sequential probabilities.
Output
The measured q for each survey. Order effects were large & significant, yet q clustered at ≈ 0 (no significant deviation) — the parameter-free prediction held across all 70.
Result: a non-fitted quantum prediction confirmed on dozens of real datasets. This is the slide that wins the “is it just philosophy?” argument.
The mechanism · interference

Quantum explanation of “irrational” choice (disjunction effect)

Pothos & Busemeyer, Proc. R. Soc. B 276:2171 (2009)

People violate the “sure-thing principle”: they’d act if they knew they won, and act if they knew they lost, but freeze when the outcome is unknown. Classically impossible.

// classical law of total probability (what SHOULD hold)
Pr(B) = Pr(A₁)·Pr(B|A₁) + Pr(A₂)·Pr(B|A₂)  ⇒ Pr(B) lies between the two

// quantum: the unknown state is a SUPERPOSITION of the two known states
Pr(B | unknown) = |ψ₁ + ψ₂|² = |ψ₁|² + |ψ₂|² + 2·Re(ψ₁*ψ₂)
// destructive interference 2·Re(ψ₁*ψ₂) < 0 pushes Pr BELOW both — the freeze

// model: 4-D space (opponent × own action); evolve under
H = Hpayoff + Hcognitive,   |ψ(t)⟩ = e−iHt|ψ₀⟩
Domain input
Behavioural choice rates from two-stage gambles & a Prisoner’s-Dilemma task under three conditions: told-win, told-lose, unknown.
Output
Fitted unitary reproduces the observed unknown-condition rate via the interference term. An equivalent classical Markov model provably cannot (it obeys the LoTP).
Result: quantum > classical on a textbook rationality violation — the core “quantum beats classical” claim.
The finance bridge

Quantum Decision Theory for risky prospects

Yukalov & Sornette, Adv. Complex Syst. 13:659 (2010); Theory and Decision 70:283 (2011)

Applies the same machinery to lotteries/prospects — the objects of prospect theory and behavioural finance. (Sornette is a famous crash-modelling econophysicist → our credible finance link.)

// probability of choosing prospect π = rational "utility" part + interference "attraction" part
Pr(π) = f(π) + q(π)
Σπ f(π) = 1,   Σπ q(π) = 0,   −1 ≤ q(π) ≤ 1

// the "quarter law": with no other info, the interference magnitude averages to 1/4
⟨ |q(π)| ⟩ ≈ 0.25  ← a parameter-free prior for irrational pull
Domain input
Choice frequencies from risk/uncertainty experiments — Allais & Ellsberg paradoxes, conjunction & disjunction effects, lottery pairs.
Output
Predicted choice probabilities including the non-classical reversals; the quarter law estimates the interference size with no fitting.
Use it for: framing the investor decision as a “prospect”, and a defensible, finance-grounded citation.
Buildability proof · on qubits

Quantum-circuit components for decision-making

Widdows, Rani & Pothos, Entropy 25(4):548 (2023)

Shows these cognitive models run as actual qubit circuits — gates and measurement, on simulators or real hardware. This is why a weekend build is realistic.

// one yes/no question = one qubit; a rotation sets the "yes" amplitude
Ry(θ) |0⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + sin(θ/2)|1⟩

// incompatible questions = non-commuting rotations ⇒ order effect
Ry(θB) · Ry... with tilted axes:  UBUA ≠ UAUB

// disjunction / interference: superpose, add relative phase, measure
H|0⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2  → phase φ → measure
Domain input
Cognitive-task parameters (rotation angles, phases) encoding a decision model.
Output
Measurement statistics that match the cognitive predictions; runnable in Qiskit / PennyLane and on NISQ hardware.
⟶ what our code actually implements

Our single-qubit investor (in /quantum_investor)

Direct, minimal instance of the formalism above — verified to satisfy QQ-equality for all parameters.
// belief state, prepared by a PennyLane circuit R_y(2α)
|ψ⟩ = cos α |0⟩ + sin α |1⟩

// Question A measured in the computational (Z) basis; Question B in a TILTED basis
|A=yes⟩ = |0⟩    |B=yes⟩ = cos β |0⟩ + sin β |1⟩

// the order effect on B's yes-rate (β = tilt = incompatibility)
Δ = cos²(α−β) − [ cos²α·cos²β + sin²α·sin²β ]  ≠ 0 when β ≠ 0

// and we verify numerically, for ALL α,β:
q = sin²β − sin²β = 0  ← the QQ-equality, parameter-free ✓
Input (domain)
Eight sequential yes/no probabilities (both orders) from humans — currently illustrative; swap in real survey data.
Output
Fitted (α, β); quantum fits ~131× better than the classical baseline; classical order effect = 0; QQ = 0. Headline figure auto-generated.

The Madness of People Is Quantum

Two famous quotes — one from Newton, one from Feynman — sound unrelated. They're actually the same sentence. And resolving them is real, published science you can run on a few qubits.

Isaac Newton · 1720

"I can calculate the motion of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people."

…said after losing a fortune in the South Sea Bubble.
⟷
Richard Feynman

"If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics."

…classical intuition is the wrong tool for some systems.

01The idea in one breath

If you remember nothing else, remember this:

Newton couldn't predict the “madness of people” for the exact same reason Feynman says you can't grasp quantum mechanics classically: human decision-making doesn't obey classical probability — it obeys quantum probability.

Newton tried to apply heavenly (deterministic, classical) mechanics to a system that is fundamentally quantum-like. The “madness” isn't random noise to average away — it's structured non-classical behaviour. Feynman tells us why classical intuition was doomed; the science of quantum cognition tells us what to use instead.

02Why human choices look “quantum”

People break the rules of classical probability in three signature ways — and these are exactly the fingerprints of quantum probability. You don't need any physics to feel why.

Order effects

Order changes the answer

Ask “Is the market safe?” then “Should you invest?” and you get different answers than asking them in the reverse order. In classical probability, order can't matter. In quantum, it's natural — measurements that don't commute.

Interference

Uncertainty isn't an average

People who'd invest after good news and after bad news often won't invest when the news is unknown. Classically impossible. Quantum-ly, the two possibilities interfere and cancel.

Context

The question creates the answer

Preferences aren't fixed values waiting to be read — how you ask constructs the choice. That's exactly what a quantum measurement does: it collapses a superposition.

Visual: why “order matters” = “the axes are tilted”

Each question is a direction. Answering = projecting your mental state onto that direction. Project onto A then B, vs B then A — because the arrows are tilted (non-commuting), you land in different places. That tilt is the whole trick.

Visual: interference of intentions → a bubble

When many investors' “intentions” line up, their quantum waves add constructively — a bubble. When they clash, they cancel — a crash of confidence. Drag the slider: this is herding as interference.

04The quantum engine: QNDM + QAE

Quantum cognition is the motivation — it says the market is genuinely a superposition (of investor intentions, of price paths) rather than one fixed classical reality. Once you accept that, you need machinery to compute on that superposition. Two quantum primitives do the heavy lifting. Each is explained high-level first, then the math.

Primitive 1 · the speedup engine

QAE — Quantum Amplitude Estimation

Brassard et al.; Montanaro, Proc. R. Soc. A 471:20150301 (2015) — quantum speedup of Monte Carlo

High level. You have a biased coin and want its bias p (read: “the average payoff of this option”). Classically you flip it N times and count — but sampling error shrinks slowly, like 1/√N, so reaching error ε costs ~1/ε² flips (one more digit = 100× the work). QAE instead encodes the bias as a rotation angle and reads that angle by quantum interference — like measuring a spinning needle instead of tallying flips. Error ε now costs ~1/ε operations: quadratically fewer (one more digit = 10×, not 100×).

// 𝒜 prepares a flag qubit c whose P[c=1] IS the quantity you want
𝒜|0⟩ = √(1−a)·|Ψ₀⟩|0⟩c  +  √a·|Ψ₁⟩|1⟩c

// here that amplitude a is exactly the (rescaled) expected payoff
a = Pr[c=1] = (1/Cmax)·𝔼[ max(f−K, 0) ]

// write a = sin²ϑ, then build the Grover operator (two reflections)
Q = 𝒜·S₀·𝒜†·Sχ  ⇒ rotation by 2ϑ, eigenvalues e±2iϑ
// the angle you want now lives in a PHASE → read it with phase estimation

// t counting qubits + controlled Q, Q², Q⁴, …, Q^(2^(t−1)) + inverse QFT
ã = sin²(πy / 2t),   |ã − a| = O(2−t) = O(ε)

// cost = Σ 2ʲ = 2ᵗ−1 = O(1/ε) calls  vs  classical Monte Carlo O(1/ε²)
quadratic speed-up: 1/ε² → 1/ε

This is the whole reason to use a quantum computer at all: the same expectation, resolved with quadratically fewer operations.

Primitive 2 · read without destroying

QNDM — Quantum Non-Demolition Measurement

Quantum non-demolition phase-kick / “detector ancilla” scheme

High level. Normally, measuring a quantum system destroys its superposition — it collapses to one outcome. That’s fatal here: our state holds all the price paths (or investor intentions) at once, and measuring it directly just collapses to one random path — which is plain classical Monte Carlo again, advantage gone. A non-demolition measurement dodges this: bolt on a cheap disposable probe qubit, let the quantity you care about imprint onto the probe’s phase, and measure the probe. The system’s superposition stays perfectly intact.

Picture it: you don’t open all the boxes to read what’s inside (that destroys the arrangement). You wave one sensor over them at once — the needle accumulates the aggregate signal, and the boxes stay closed.
// detector ancilla starts in |+⟩
|+⟩d = ( |0⟩ + |1⟩ ) / √2

// a controlled phase kicks the payoff f(x) into the detector's PHASE
// the path register |x⟩ is NEVER collapsed — this is "non-demolition"
(1/√2) Σx √p(x) |x⟩ ( |0⟩d + e iλf(x) |1⟩d )

// measure ONLY the detector in the X basis:
Pr[0] = ½ ( 1 + Re G(λ) ),   G(λ) = 𝔼[ e iλ(f−K) ]

// G(λ) = characteristic function = a property of the WHOLE distribution
// sweep λ, then Gil–Peláez Fourier-invert → 𝔼[ max(f−K, 0) ]

One qubit’s measurement statistics hand you a distribution-level quantity, without ever projecting onto a single path.

How the two fit together

They answer different questions, and the three “routes” in the option-pricing paper are just different ways of marrying them:

QNDM gives you
How to read information off the giant superposition without destroying it — the phase trick. Cheap: one ancilla, no value register.
QAE gives you
How to turn an expectation into an amplitude and estimate it quadratically faster — the 1/ε² → 1/ε speed-up engine.
Fourier route. QNDM alone — cheap on qubits, but you must sweep λ and Fourier-invert.
QAE-amplitude route. Amplitude alone — one shot, no λ sweep, but spends extra qubits computing the payoff into a register.
QSVT route. The ambitious fusion — keeps QNDM’s cheap phase trick and QAE’s single-shot estimate, via a spectral transform of the phase oracle.

05What we actually build & show

The motivation says the market is non-classical, so we stop fighting it and compute with it: a quantum option pricer that loads every price path into a real superposition and reads the fair price off it — beating classical Monte Carlo by a quadratic factor, with no expensive distribution-loading oracle.

The on-stage story (5 beats)

The lament. Newton's South Sea Bubble loss + his quote. The father of classical physics, wrecked by a market he couldn't price.
The market is a superposition. A price path is up/down at each step — so the whole future is a superposition of 2M paths. We load it exactly & for free: one Ry rotation per step (a product distribution, no loading oracle).
Read the price without collapsing it. QNDM kicks the payoff into a single ancilla's phase; QAE turns the expected payoff into an amplitude. Feynman's quote drops here — we measure the whole distribution, never one path.
Quadratic speed-up. 1/ε² → 1/ε versus Monte Carlo, and we skip the loading oracle that dominates prior quantum pricers (Stamatopoulos 2020).
Finance payoff. Prices European and path-dependent Asian options, with time-dependent volatility/rates handled natively — exactly the hard, high-value cases OP cares about.

Tech (small & feasible)

  • ~M+1 qubits for M time steps: M path qubits + 1 detector ancilla (+ a small work register for the QAE route).
  • Step 0 classical: binomial tree (Cox–Ross–Rubinstein) → risk-neutral up-probabilities qi → rotation angles θi=2·arcsin√qi.
  • Demo route: QNDM + Fourier (cheapest qubits) for a European call at small M, then the Asian payoff to show path-dependence.
  • Classical baseline = Monte-Carlo pricer + closed-form Black–Scholes, to show the same price at quadratically more samples.
  • Stretch: the single-run QAE route and the QSVT fusion (§04); time-dependent σ(t), r(t).
  • Stack: PennyLane or Qiskit, statevector simulator, Python plots. No real quantum hardware needed.
